Korean has 19 consonants. They come in three types: basic (평음), aspirated (격음 — extra breath), and tense (경음 — tensed throat). Each consonant has a name and a position in the syllable block.
Basic Consonants 평음
Aspirated Consonants 격음 — with a puff of air
Tense Consonants 경음 — tensed, no air
The tense consonants (ㄲ ㄸ ㅃ ㅆ ㅉ) might sound like the basic ones at first, but they're produced with a tightened throat and no breath. Think of the difference between "p" in "spin" (no puff) vs "p" in "pin" (with puff) — Korean distinguishes these systematically.
Korean has 21 vowels — 10 basic and 11 compound vowels formed by combining basics. Vowels always pair with a consonant in a syllable block. When a vowel starts a syllable, ㅇ acts as a silent placeholder.
Basic Vowels 기본 모음
Compound Vowels 이중 모음 — combinations
The vowel ㅡ (eu) has no equivalent in English or Filipino — it's pronounced with the mouth flat and unrounded, like the sound you make when someone pokes you in the stomach. Practice this one separately!
A 받침 (batchim) is a consonant placed at the bottom of a syllable block. Not all syllables have one — it's optional. But when present, it changes the sound of the syllable. There are 27 possible batchim, but only 7 actual sounds they make.
Even though there are 27 batchim spellings, they all collapse into just 7 sounds: ㄱ ㄴ ㄷ ㄹ ㅁ ㅂ ㅇ. For example ㅋ, ㄲ, ㄱ all sound like "k" at the end of a syllable. This makes it much easier than it looks!

At first, Korean blocks might look like a single complex character, but they're always made of the same letters you already know! Train your eye to spot the individual pieces: top-left = initial consonant, right or bottom = vowel, bottom = batchim (if any).
Korean is written left to right, top to bottom — same as English and Filipino. But within each syllable block, the stroke order follows specific rules. Learning them from the start builds good habits.
Stroke Order Rules
1
Top to bottom — Always write higher strokes before lower ones. ㅏ: write the vertical line first, then the horizontal tick.
2
Left to right — When strokes are side by side, write the left one first. ㄱ: horizontal stroke first (left→right), then vertical stroke down.
3
Horizontal before vertical — When strokes cross, write the horizontal line first. ㅗ: horizontal line first, then the vertical tick up.
4
Center before sides — For symmetric characters like ㅁ, draw the outer frame then fill in. ㅂ: two verticals first, then horizontals.
5
Inside before closing — For enclosed shapes, write the inside strokes before the closing stroke. ㅎ: top hat → circle → cross stroke.
Linking Sounds (연음)
When batchim meets a vowel 연음법칙 · Linking Rule

When a syllable ends in a batchim and the next syllable starts with ㅇ (silent), the batchim sound moves to the next syllable.

Written
먹어요
meok + eo + yo
Pronounced
meo·geo·yo
ㄱ slides to next syllable
This linking rule is why romanization can look different from how a word is spelled in Hangul. 먹어요 is spelled with ㄱ at the end of 먹, but pronounced "meo·geo·yo" because the ㄱ links forward. Focus on listening carefully — your ear will catch it faster than your eyes!
