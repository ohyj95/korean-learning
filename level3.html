COMPARISON

How Korean grammar works — compared side by side with English and Filipino (Tagalog). Understanding the differences is the fastest way to stop making mistakes.

🇰🇷 한국어 Korean
🇺🇸 English
🇵🇭 Filipino / Tagalog
This is the most important difference between Korean and English/Filipino. Korean puts the verb at the END of the sentence — always. Everything else comes before it. Once you internalize this, Korean sentences will start to feel natural.
Basic Sentence Structure
Subject · Object · Verb
한국어 Korean
Subject + Object + Verb
Example 나는 밥을 먹어요. Na-neun bab-eul meo-geo-yo.

SOV order. The verb always comes last. Even in long sentences with many clauses, the main verb is at the very end.

English
Subject + Verb + Object
I eat rice.

SVO order. The verb always follows the subject directly. This is what makes English feel so different from Korean.

Filipino / Tagalog
Verb + Subject + Object
Kumakain ako ng kanin.

VSO order. Filipino puts the verb FIRST — the exact opposite of Korean. This is actually the biggest adjustment for Filipino learners.

For Filipino speakers, the jump to Korean word order is the hardest part — not Hangul. In Tagalog you say "Kumain ako ng mansanas" (Ate I apple), but in Korean you'd say "나는 사과를 먹었어요" (I apple ate). Practice by mentally flipping your Tagalog sentences: move the verb from front to back.
Adjectives Come BEFORE Nouns
All three languages agree here
한국어 Korean
Adjective + Noun
예쁜 꽃 Ye-ppeun kkot

Pretty flower. Adjectives precede the noun, same as English.

English
Adjective + Noun
Pretty flower.

Same as Korean — adjective before noun.

Filipino / Tagalog
Adjective na / Noun
Magandang bulaklak.

Filipino also puts adjectives before nouns, but uses a linker na / -ng to connect them.

Korean uses particles — small grammatical markers attached to nouns — to show the role of each word in a sentence. English does this through word order. Filipino uses prepositions (sa, ng, si) which are somewhat similar to Korean particles.
Filipino learners have an advantage here! Tagalog particles like si, ang, ng, sa work similarly to Korean particles. "Si Maria" (subject marker) → like Korean 이/가. "Ng" (object marker) → like Korean 을/를.

Korean verbs always come at the end of the sentence and change form based on tense, formality, and mood. Unlike English, Korean verbs don't change based on the subject (no "he goes" vs "I go"). Unlike Filipino, Korean verbs don't encode who the focus of action is.
Present / Past / Future
Verb Tense
먹다 · To eat · Kumain
한국어 Korean
Present 먹어요 Meo-geo-yo
Past 먹었어요 Meo-geo-sseo-yo
Future 먹을 거예요 Meo-geul geo-ye-yo

Verb endings change. The -았/었- infix marks past tense. -ㄹ 거예요 marks future.

English
Present I eat / I am eating
Past I ate / I was eating
Future I will eat

Irregular past forms ("eat → ate"). Auxiliary verbs used for future ("will"). Continuous forms with "-ing".

Filipino / Tagalog
Present Kumakain ako
Past Kumain ako
Future Kakain ako

Aspect (not tense) is marked by reduplication and infixes. "Kumakain" (ongoing) vs "kumain" (completed).

Formal vs Casual Speech
Speech Levels
Korean has multiple formality levels built into verbs
한국어 Korean
Formal (to elders/strangers) 먹습니다 Meok-seum-ni-da
Polite (everyday) 먹어요 Meo-geo-yo
Casual (friends) 먹어 Meo-geo

Using the wrong level is a social mistake. Start with -아/어요 (polite) — it works in most situations.

English
Formal One eats / I consume
Polite I eat (please / thank you)
Casual I eat / I'm eating

English handles formality through vocabulary choice and politeness words ("please", "would you mind"), not verb forms.

Filipino / Tagalog
Formal / Polite Kumakain po ako
Polite Kumakain ako
Casual Kumakain ako / Kumakain lang

Filipino uses the particle po/ho to add respect — similar in spirit to Korean formality levels. This will feel familiar!

The Filipino po / ho system is very close to how Korean speech levels work — both are ways of showing respect to elders and strangers through language. In Korean, this respect is baked into the verb ending itself rather than a separate particle.
Making sentences negative works very differently across the three languages. Korean has two ways to negate — a short form and a long form — and both go at the end of the sentence (because everything does in Korean).
Negation
"I don't eat" / "Hindi kumakain"
한국어 Korean
Short form — 안 (before verb) 안 먹어요 An meo-geo-yo
Long form — -지 않아요 (after verb stem) 먹지 않아요 Meok-ji a-na-yo

안 goes directly before the verb (short, casual).
-지 않아요 attaches to the verb stem (longer, more formal).
Both mean the same thing.

English
With auxiliary "do" I don't eat.
With "not" I do not eat.

English requires the auxiliary "do/does/did" to negate. "Not" goes after the auxiliary, before the main verb. Past: "I didn't eat."

Filipino / Tagalog
Hindi (general negation) Hindi ako kumakain.
Wala (absence/none) Wala akong pagkain.

Hindi is placed before the verb phrase — similar to Korean 안 in position. Wala is used for "there is none" / "I have none".

Filipino "Hindi" and Korean "안" work similarly — both are placed before the verb to negate it. The big difference: in Korean the verb still goes to the END, so "안" sits just before the verb at the end: 나는 밥을 안 먹어요 (I rice not eat).
Forming questions in Korean is remarkably simple. You mostly just change the ending of the sentence — the word order stays exactly the same as a statement. No subject-verb inversion needed (unlike English).
Yes/No Questions
Statement → Question
한국어 Korean
Statement 밥을 먹어요. Bab-eul meo-geo-yo.
Question (rising intonation) 밥을 먹어요? Bab-eul meo-geo-yo?

Same words, same order! Just add ? and raise your intonation. Easy.

English
Statement You eat rice.
Question (inverted) Do you eat rice?

English requires subject-verb inversion AND an auxiliary verb "do". More complex than Korean or Filipino.

Filipino / Tagalog
Statement Kumakain ka ng kanin.
Question (ba marker) Kumakain ka ba ng kanin?

Filipino uses the question particle ba — placed after the first word of the sentence. Very clean system!

Wh- Questions
Who · What · Where · When · Why · How
한국어 Korean
뭐 먹어요? Mwo meo-geo-yo?

누구 (nu-gu) — Who
뭐 / 무엇 (mwo) — What
어디 (eo-di) — Where
언제 (eon-je) — When
왜 (wae) — Why
어떻게 (eo-tteo-ke) — How

Question words go where the answer would go — not at the front like English.

English
What do you eat?

Who — Who
What — What
Where — Where
When — When
Why — Why
How — How

Question words go to the FRONT of the sentence, then verb inversion follows.

Filipino / Tagalog
Ano ang kinakain mo?

Sino — Who
Ano — What
Saan — Where
Kailan — When
Bakit — Why
Paano — How

Question words go at the beginning, similar to English. The verb still comes first in the rest of the sentence.

Korean has a built-in honorific system that affects vocabulary, verb endings, and even pronouns. This isn't just politeness — it's a grammatical system. Using the wrong level with the wrong person is a real social faux pas. Filipino and English handle respect very differently.
How Respect Is Expressed
Social hierarchy in language
한국어 Korean
Casual (to friend) 밥 먹었어? Bab meo-geo-sseo?
Polite (to adult/stranger) 밥 드셨어요? Bab deu-syeo-sseo-yo?

The verb itself changes: 먹다 (eat, casual) → 드시다 (eat, honorific). Even the noun "rice" can change: 밥 → 진지 in very formal contexts.

English
Casual Did you eat?
Polite Have you had a chance to eat?

English has no grammatical honorific system. Formality is shown through vocabulary choice, longer phrasing, and tone. Words don't change their form based on social hierarchy.

Filipino / Tagalog
Casual Kumain ka na ba?
Polite (po/ho) Kumain na po kayo?

Filipino uses po / ho (respect particle) and kayo (plural "you" used as formal singular) — both familiar concepts for understanding Korean honorifics.

The Filipino kayo used as a respectful "you" is exactly like how Korean uses different pronouns for formality. And just as Filipinos say "po" to elders, Koreans use verb endings like -요 / -습니다 to signal respect. This cultural instinct — adjusting language for social hierarchy — is shared by both cultures, which is a huge advantage!
