Korean has two number systems — and knowing which to use (and when) is one of the trickiest parts of the language. Master this and everyday Korean becomes dramatically easier.

Number

Korean has two completely separate number systems that coexist and are both used daily. Sino-Korean (한자어) numbers come from Chinese, while Native Korean (순우리말) numbers are purely Korean in origin. The rule for which to use depends entirely on the context — not your preference.

Native Korean numbers 하나, 둘, 셋, 넷, 스물 change form before a counter word: 하나→한, 둘→두, 셋→세, 넷→네, 스물→스무. So it's 한 개 (one thing) not 하나 개, and 스무 살 (age 20) not 스물 살. This is one of the most common beginner mistakes!
Notice that 일흔(70), 여든(80), 아흔(90) start with the same syllable as the single digit — 일곱(7)→일흔, 여덟(8)→여든, 아홉(9)→아흔. Use this to remember them! Same trick for Filipino: "pito" (7) and "pitumpu" (70) share a root — Korean works the same way for the higher tens.
In Korean, you can't just say a number — you need to attach a counter word (단위사) that matches the type of thing you're counting. This is similar to English "two cups of coffee" or "three sheets of paper" — but in Korean it's required for everything. Almost all counters use Native Korean numbers.
Tagalog also uses counters! "Dalawang piraso ng tinapay" (two pieces of bread) is exactly the same concept. The difference is Korean has many more specific counters — one for animals, one for books, one for bottles, one for flat things, etc.

Word order: [Noun] + [Number] + [Counter] — The number and counter come AFTER the noun, not before like in English. "Two apples" = 사과 (apple) + 두 (two) + 개 (counter) = 사과 두 개
Korean counts in units of 만 (10,000) rather than thousands like English. This is the biggest source of confusion when dealing with Korean prices, population numbers, or statistics. Once you get the 만 unit, everything clicks.

Korean prices feel huge at first — a coffee for "사천오백 원" sounds like 4,500 of something scary. But ₩4,500 is about ₱170 or $3.40. The trick: when you see 원 prices, just mentally divide by ~1,300 to get USD. Fluent readers just recognize price patterns without translating — you'll get there too!

Here's where the two number systems collide in the same sentence. Hours (시) use Native Korean numbers, but minutes (분) use Sino-Korean. Dates use Sino-Korean throughout. And Korean age has its own unique rules.
Telling Time
시 (hours) = Native Korean numbers · 분 (minutes) = Sino-Korean numbers
So "3:30" = 세 시 (native) + 삼십 분 (sino) = 세 시 삼십 분

Korean Age System 나이
Two Age Systems both still used in daily life
SystemRuleExample (born Jan 1, 2000)
세는 나이
Traditional age		 Born as 1. Add 1 every New Year's Day (Jan 1). Everyone ages at the same time. 27살
in 2026
만 나이
International age (official since 2023)		 Born as 0. Add 1 on your birthday. Same as the Western system. 25 또는 26살
depending on birthday month
Since June 2023, Korea officially adopted 만 나이 (international age) for legal and administrative purposes. But in daily conversation, many Koreans still use the traditional system — especially older generations. When a Korean asks "몇 살이에요?" (how old are you?), it's safe to answer with your international age now.
Two very practical contexts where numbers come up constantly in Korea: phone numbers and prices. Both use Sino-Korean, but with slightly different reading conventions.
Phone Numbers 전화번호
Reading Phone Numbers
Phone numbers are read digit by digit using Sino-Korean. Note: 0 = 공 (gong) — not 영 (yeong). Also, 6 softens to 육→유 in some contexts.

How Korean, English, and Filipino handle numbers differently — and where Filipino speakers have an advantage (or an extra hurdle) when learning Korean numbers.
Number System Structure
🇰🇷 Korean
두 가지 체계
du ga-ji che-gye
Two systems (Sino + Native). Which to use depends on context — fixed rules.
🇺🇸 English
One system
One number system only. No counters required (just "two apples").
🇵🇭 Filipino
Dalawang sistema
Often mixes Tagalog (isa, dalawa) + Spanish-derived (dose, trenta) + English. Context-driven like Korean!
Counters / Classifiers
🇰🇷 Korean
개, 마리, 권, 잔...
Required for every noun. Specific counter for each category of thing.
🇺🇸 English
Optional
"Two dogs" not "two [animal] dogs". Counters exist but optional: "two cups of water".
🇵🇭 Filipino
piraso, butil, tasa...
Used similarly to Korean! "Dalawang piraso ng tinapay" = two pieces of bread. Familiar concept!
Grouping Units
🇰🇷 Korean
만 (10,000)
Groups by ten-thousands. 100,000 = 10만 (ten ten-thousands). Very different from English!
🇺🇸 English
Thousand (1,000)
Groups by thousands. 100,000 = one hundred thousand. Commas every 3 digits.
🇵🇭 Filipino
libo (1,000)
Groups by thousands like English. "Sampung libo" = ten thousand. Same unit issue as English speakers when learning Korean.
Age Counting
🇰🇷 Korean
만 나이 (official)
세는 나이 (traditional)
Two systems coexist. Traditional: born as 1, ages on Jan 1. Official since 2023: same as Western.
🇺🇸 English
International age
Born as 0, add 1 on birthday. Simple and universal.
🇵🇭 Filipino
International age
Same as English system. No special age counting — so the Korean traditional system will feel strange at first!
Filipino learners have a real advantage with counters — you already use "piraso, tasa, pares" naturally. The mindset of attaching a classifier to counted nouns is familiar. The hard part is learning which Korean counter matches which category, and remembering that 개 is your safe default when unsure!
